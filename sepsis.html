Lung Analysis in Sepsis Patients

Project summary • clinical data analysis • ARDS stratification • P/F ratio methodology

Sepsis is a life-threatening complication resulting from the body’s extreme response to infection. Instead of localizing the infection, the immune system overreacts, causing tissue damage, organ dysfunction, and potentially death.

Impact on the Lungs

Clinical focus

Sepsis often affects the lungs, causing Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome (ARDS) where inflammation and fluid accumulation reduce oxygen transfer. Key consequences:

  • Breathing difficulty
  • Rapidly falling oxygen levels
  • Risk of respiratory failure if untreated

Lung analysis allows clinicians to:

  • Detect abnormal biomarkers
  • Monitor respiratory changes over time
  • Guide treatment and ICU decisions

n = 40,336 patients

Methodology

PaO₂ → P/F → ARDS

Step 1 — Convert SpO₂ to PaO₂

  • SpO₂ 100–90%: ↓1% → PaO₂ ↓ 4 mmHg
  • SpO₂ 90–80%: ↓1% → PaO₂ ↓ 1.5 mmHg
  • SpO₂ <80%: PaO₂ ≈ 50% of SpO₂
CASE TRUE
  WHEN [O2Sat] BETWEEN 90 AND 100 THEN (100 - (100 - [O2Sat]) * 4)
  WHEN [O2Sat] BETWEEN 80 AND 89  THEN (60 - ((90 - [O2Sat]) * 1.5))
  WHEN [O2Sat] < 80              THEN ([O2Sat] / 2)
END

Step 2 — P/F Ratio

P/F Ratio = PaO₂ (mmHg) / FiO₂

Step 3 — ARDS Classification

≥ 300 — No ARDS
200–299 — Mild ARDS
100–199 — Moderate ARDS
<100 — Severe ARDS

Key Findings

Clinical insights
  • FiO₂ and RESP are the strongest indicators of lung dysfunction.
  • Early lung issues → longer ICU stays; late-stage ARDS → rapid decline.
  • Acid–base imbalances vary by ICU type.
  • Males show higher leukocytosis than females.
Early monitoring of lung markers and acid–base status guides ICU decisions and supports timely intervention in sepsis care.
